Detail & Texture

The four tools that affect perceived sharpness and crispness
Making a subject crisper: Texture → Clarity → Sharpening work at different scales. Start with Texture for fine surface detail, add Clarity if you want more punch and 3D separation, then refine with Sharpening in the Detail panel. Dehaze is for large-scale atmospheric contrast only.
The four crispness tools compared
Texture
Presence panel
Enhances micro surface detail — the very fine, high-frequency structure in an image. Adds crispness without affecting smooth areas or overall contrast.
Scale
Fine
fur & feathers skin pores bark & rock fabric smooth bokeh bg
Hold Alt/Option while dragging to see a grayscale preview of what's being affected.
Clarity
Presence panel
Adds midtone contrast at a medium scale — makes edges pop and gives a more "3D" or punchy look. Affects a wider radius than Texture. Negative clarity gives a soft, dreamy glow effect.
Scale
Medium
subject separation drama / grit landscapes smooth skin portraits anything overdone
Texture handles skin better than Clarity — prefer Texture if you're trying to crisp up a face without making pores harsh.
Sharpening
Detail panel
True pixel-level edge sharpening. Works at the smallest scale. Controlled by four sub-sliders (Amount, Radius, Detail, Masking). Affects the whole image unless you use the Masking slider.
Scale
Pixel
global sharpening export prep high ISO noise (use Masking)
Alt/Option + drag Masking to see the mask. White = sharpened. Raise it until smooth areas turn black so noise doesn't get sharpened.
Dehaze
Presence panel
Removes atmospheric haze by boosting large-scale contrast and color saturation. Positive = cuts haze. Negative = adds haze / ethereal fog. Works at the largest scale of the four.
Scale
Large
foggy landscapes underwater shots dramatic sky (local) portraits indoor / studio
Use on a local mask (sky or background only) rather than globally — full-image Dehaze quickly looks harsh.

Tone

Six sliders that map to different regions of the histogram
Think of it as the histogram in zones: Blacks → Shadows → Exposure → Highlights → Whites runs from the darkest pixel to the brightest. Exposure is the anchor for the whole image; Whites/Blacks set the hard endpoints; Highlights/Shadows recover compressed detail.
Slider What zone it affects Best use Tip
Exposure Entire histogram shifts together Getting the base brightness right first — adjust this before anything else Think of it as the aperture equivalent in post. Everything moves together.
Contrast Applies an S-curve — darkens darks, brightens brights simultaneously Quick drama boost, but can crush shadows/blow highlights if overdone More control to use the Tone Curve directly instead of this slider.
Highlights Bright tones — roughly the top 1/3 of the histogram Pulling back sky and bright areas without touching the whole image Highlights has a gentler reach than Whites. Use Highlights first, then Whites to clip.
Whites The very brightest tones — the white point (clipping) Setting where pure white lands. Positive clips to white; negative pulls back blow-outs Alt/Option + drag to see clipping preview. No colour = clipping to pure white.
Shadows Dark-to-mid tones — roughly the bottom 1/3 of the histogram Opening up shadow detail, pulling texture out of dark areas Raising Shadows can quickly look flat/grey. Counteract with a slight Blacks reduction.
Blacks The very darkest tones — the black point Setting how deep the darkest shadow goes. Dropping it adds richness/depth. Classic combo: raise Shadows + lower Blacks to open detail without looking washed out.
Highlights vs Whites — the most confused pair
Highlights
Affects a broad range of bright tones. Pulling it down recovers detail in bright but not blown areas — clouds, bright fabric, a lit face. Gentler and more gradual in its transition.
cloud recovery bright skin window light
Whites
Only affects the absolute brightest pixels — where clipping happens. Use positive to intentionally clip to white (high-key look). Use negative to pull back actual blow-outs in RAW.
setting white point recovering blown RAW data lifting midtones (use Exposure)
Shadows vs Blacks — the other confused pair
Shadows
Lifts the dark-mid zone — the murky region above pure black. Great for pulling texture and detail out of dark areas without making blacks look grey.
shadow detail recovery backlit subjects
Blacks
Sets the black point — where the darkest thing in your image sits. Dropping it deepens the darkest tones and adds richness. Counteracts the flat look of raised Shadows.
depth and richness after raising Shadows

Color

White balance, saturation, HSL, and tone curve colour
Vibrance vs Saturation — always use Vibrance first
Vibrance
Presence panel
Smart saturation — boosts less-saturated colours more aggressively while protecting colours that are already vivid, and specifically protecting skin-tone ranges. Almost always the better choice.
portraits adding pop without garishness any image really
Vibrance of +20 looks natural. Saturation +20 often looks overdone.
Saturation
Presence panel
Boosts all colours equally and globally. Can quickly push already-vivid colours into oversaturation. Useful for desaturation (pulling colour out) — negative values reduce all colours equally.
global desaturation black & white conversion (−100) positive on portraits already vivid landscapes
White Balance — set this first, everything else depends on it
SliderAxisWhat it doesCommon fix
Temperature Blue ←→ Orange Shifts the overall warmth. Higher = warmer/more orange. Lower = cooler/more blue. Overcast sky makes shots too blue — raise Temperature. Tungsten light too orange — lower it.
Tint Green ←→ Magenta Corrects green or magenta colour casts. Usually needed after setting Temperature. Fluorescent lights add green — raise Tint toward magenta. Mixed lighting often needs both sliders.
HSL — per-colour control (Hue, Saturation, Luminance)
ChannelWhat it controlsExample use
Hue Shifts the actual colour — e.g. push Orange more toward Yellow, or Blue toward Cyan Sunset sky looking too red — shift Reds and Oranges toward yellow to get that golden look
Saturation Intensity of that specific colour only Sky too neon — drop Aqua/Blue saturation. Or boost just the greens in foliage.
Luminance Brightness of pixels of that colour Make the sky darker without touching anything else — lower Blue luminance. Brightening teeth — raise Yellow luminance.
Use the target/bullseye icon to click directly on a colour in your image and drag up/down — faster than guessing which colour range to adjust.
Tone Curve vs Basic Panel
Basic Panel Tone
Sliders are intelligent — they protect the rest of the tonal range while adjusting their zone. Fast, non-destructive starting point. Most adjustments can be done entirely here.
primary editingmost images
Tone Curve
Precise control over any tonal zone. Drag a point to affect only that region. Also has per-channel (R/G/B) curves for coloured shadows/highlights. Film looks, faded lifts, split-toning all start here.
precise tonal controlfilm / matte looksplit toning
Lift the bottom-left point (shadow point) off the floor to create a faded/matte look.

Noise Reduction

Luminance vs colour noise — different problems, different fixes
Rule of thumb: Always fix Colour noise first (it's free — doesn't cost sharpness). Then deal with Luminance noise, which trades detail for smoothness.
Luminance Noise
Detail panel → Noise Reduction
Smooths out the speckly grey grain in shadows at high ISO. Reduces sharpness as you raise it — there's always a trade-off. Use the Detail and Contrast sliders to recover some of what you lose.
high ISO grain shadow speckle
Detail slider: higher preserves texture but keeps more noise. Contrast slider: higher keeps tonal contrast but can look mottled. Balance against Amount.
Colour Noise
Detail panel → Noise Reduction
Removes the coloured blotches — magenta, green, blue speckles — that appear at high ISO, especially in shadows. High values rarely hurt sharpness. Default of 25 is usually enough; go to 50–70 for serious high-ISO work.
colour blotching high ISO shadows
Detail slider here controls how much edge detail is preserved — raising it keeps colour edges sharper but can leave fringing. Default is fine in most cases.
AI Denoise (Lightroom 2023+)
Denoise (AI)
Photo → Enhance → Denoise
Machine learning denoising that beats the Detail panel's noise reduction significantly. Creates a new DNG file. Best used when Luminance NR at full strength is still leaving too much noise or costing too much detail. Not a real-time slider — takes seconds to process.
extreme high ISO wildlife / low light

Masking & Local Adjustments

When global sliders aren't enough — isolate what you're adjusting
Masks stack: You can add, subtract, and intersect masks. Start with an AI mask (Subject or Sky), then subtract areas that aren't right. This is faster than painting from scratch.
AI Masks — start here
Mask typeWhat it selectsBest for
SubjectAI-detected main subject(s)Brightening or sharpening your subject independently of the background
SkyAI-detected sky regionPulling back sky exposure, adding Dehaze to sky only, colour grading sky
BackgroundEverything except the subjectBlurring, desaturating, or darkening backgrounds
PeopleSub-masks: face skin, body skin, eyebrows, eyes, lips, teeth, hairPortrait retouching — brighten teeth, sharpen eyes, soften skin — each independently
ObjectsClick on any object to select itIsolating specific elements that aren't caught by Subject
Range Masks — select by property
Luminance Range
Selects pixels by brightness. Great for targeting just the shadows, just the highlights, or the midtones without affecting anything else.
pulling back blown highlights lifting shadow detail midtone contrast
Colour Range
Selects pixels by colour using an eyedropper. Click on a colour in the image to select that range. Useful for adjustments that apply only to a specific colour — sky blues, foliage greens, skin tones.
sky colour grading selective colour shift foliage

Sharpening Sliders

Detail panel — four sliders that work together
SliderWhat it doesStarting pointAlt/Option preview
Amount How much sharpening is applied overall 40–80 for most images. Higher for wildlife/birds, lower for smooth skin. Grayscale view of sharpening applied (white = sharp edges)
Radius The width of the sharpening halo around edges 0.8–1.0 for fine detail (fur, feathers). 1.5–2.0 for broader edges (landscapes, architecture). Grayscale showing radius width
Detail How much fine texture vs just clean edges gets sharpened Low (25) = edges only. High (75+) = all texture sharpened (also sharpens noise). Areas being detected for sharpening highlighted
Masking Restricts sharpening to only edge areas — smooth areas (skin, sky, bokeh) are excluded 0 = sharpen everything. 50 = only harder edges. 80+ = only the most defined edges. Raise this on noisy images. White = will be sharpened. Black = protected. This is the most useful preview — use it every time.
Starting recipe for wildlife / birds: Amount 70 · Radius 0.9 · Detail 40 · Masking 50–70. Then use Alt/Option + Masking to make sure smooth backgrounds are masked out before raising Amount.

Edit Order

Doing things in the wrong order wastes time and creates rework
#StepWhy here
1White Balance (Temperature + Tint)Everything downstream — colour, saturation, tone — depends on correct WB. Fix it first or you'll chase your tail.
2ExposureSet the overall brightness anchor before making fine adjustments.
3Highlights + Whites / Shadows + BlacksRecover or clip the tonal range now so Contrast and Tone Curve work on a good foundation.
4Contrast / Tone CurveAdd contrast after the tonal range is set, not before.
5Presence (Texture, Clarity, Vibrance)These affect how the image reads visually — don't add them until tone is right or you'll over-correct.
6HSL / Colour GradingAdjust individual colours only after global colour (WB) is correct.
7Detail panel (Noise Reduction, then Sharpening)Fix noise before adding sharpening, otherwise you sharpen the noise.
8Masking / Local AdjustmentsFine-tune specific areas after the global edit is finished.
9Lens Corrections + TransformCan be done early but usually last since they're often set as defaults.
10CropCrop last so all adjustments were applied to the full image.
